Sanibel Island, FL Back to Destinations

Overview

Sanibel Island is one of my most favorite frequented destinations due to its vibrant range of colors, local identity, and generally nice weather year-round. Nearly all the shops, ranging from ice cream to art to dining, are locally operated and provide a pleasant escape from the various chains that are sometimes too ubiquitous in many other areas.

While this destination can be quite expensive to stay at, the experiences are certainly worthwhile, and there tends to be enough flexibility in terms of rooming options to lower the cost of one's stay if planned in advance. Overall, for anyone looking for an alternative to other destinations like the Bahamas or even other localities in Florida, I would highly recommend Sanibel Island for your next stay!

Favorite Things to Do

Sanibel Island has a wide range of biodiversity due to the vastly untouched parts of the island for conservation. After taking a guided fishing trip to various locations around Sanibel Island, such as the pier, areas adjacent to the Causeway, and popular ecosystems outside of Captiva (north of Sanibel Island), we were able to ecstatically catch a few sharks and even see dolphins cruising behind ships!

On the boat dock as visualized, several pelicans would boisterously crowd around those who returned with fish, which was peculiar yet very amusing to see. Even for those not watching to fish, Sanibel Island offers several excursions similar like kayaking, where our group was able to paddle by multiple manatees floating near the surface!

Beyond this, Sanibel Island's natural beauty ensures a sense of familiarity yet diversity for every subsequent visit, regardless of the part of the island experienced. For instance, shells are naturally clustered due to the large amount of the coast facing south, thereby causing daily rain and other events to push the shells with perfect ubiquity across the coast. The sand is also a very soft and granular material, and you'd be hard to not find runners every morning due to the perfect length of the coast for a nice morning run. Overall, spending time at the beach always feels rejuvenating and manageable to due to the aforementioned smaller size of the coast.

In terms of dining, Sanibel Island, as noted, mostly contains locally operated restaurants, which tends to incentivize more variety and higher quality meals for tourists. Nearly every food, ranging from pizza to seafood to breakfast, all encompass Sanibel Island's selections and certainly my own personal recommendations from my visits. This emphasis on locality makes Sanibel Island one of the most destinations in the world in this regard!

Tips

Below, I've included a few potentially good-to-know tips that can assist you with familiarizing with Sanibel Island and a few of its intracacies:

  • Whenever you want to travel across the Causeway into Sanibel Island, you'll have to pay a subsidiary toll fee that can quickly add up if not considered. Buying groceries is cheaper off the island, but I would recommend stocking up half or least all of the groceries you'll need at chains like Publix or Walmart before making another trip.
  • You can find dolphins and manatees swimming quite close to the shores at times; I had more success when finding places near the lighhouse, and time of day isn't much of a factor unless there's a considerable amount of tourists residing further out in the water.
  • Restaurants become increasingly packed from 6-8pm, regardless of the day of the week. Early dinners may be advised so that you avoid these crowds, receive more early-bird specials, and have opportunities to do other time-sensitive activities that may otherwise be closed later on during these meal times.
  • The southeastern part of the island tends to offer cheaper hotel prices than the west side due to its proximity to Captiva. Many houses also rent out through local vacation rental services, which can offer one month housing stay prices comparable to much shorter one week cruises.
